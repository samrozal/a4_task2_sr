1. Introduction

This report provides an exploratory overview into differences in weights and sizes of juvenile snowshoe hares observed at the Bonanza Creek Long Term Ecological Research site (a portion of the US Long Term Ecological Research Network) between the years of 1998 and 2012. Specifically, we analyze the differences in juvenile snowshoe hare weights between sexes (male and female) and experimental sites (Bonanza Riparian, Bonanza Mature, and Bonanza Black Spruce). We also farther explore the relationship between juvenile hare weight and juvenile hare hind foot length by building a simple linear regression model to explore this relationship

2. Data and Methods

Measurements pertaining to snowshoe hare sizes were collected by Dr. Knut Kielland, Dr. Staurt Chapin, Dr. Roger Russ, and their team between the years of 1998 and 2012 in the Bonanza Creek Experimental Forest in Alaska, USA. The data covers 5 sites (three of which are explored further in this report, as this report focuses on Juvenile snowshoe hares) where snowshoe hares were captured in traps, measured, and then released back into the the ecological reserve.Exploratory data visualization is first conducted in order to determine which variables warrant further investigation. Following this initial exploration, the difference between male and female weights is analysed using a Welch two-sample t-test (with a significance level of 0.05), the effect size of the model is found, and a linear regression model exploring weight and hind foot lengths is constructed.

D. Relationship Between Weight and Hind Foot Length

We analyzed the relationship between juvenile hare weight (grams) and hind foot length (mm) across all juvenile hares observed during the study period. Although the relationship provides valuable insight , there is a necessity for further analysis that also includes sex of the juvenile hare and the site at which the hares were observed/trapped as variables, as these are likely to also have an impact on hare weight.

Figure 3. Relationship between juvenile snowshoe hare weight and hind foot length Individual observations are represented by points on the graph. The regression model is indicated by the dashed blue line which folows the equation: weight = 9.52g mm -1 * (hind foot length) + -279.34g (p=0.02, R2 = 0.299 Pearson’s r =0.55) Data: Kielland et al. (2017)

There is a significant linear relationship in which juvenile hare weight can be predicted by juvenile hare hind foot length (p=0.02, R2 =0.299). The slope value of \(\beta\) = 9.52 mm/g indicates a predicted increase of 9.52 g in juvenile snowshoe hare weight for a 1 millimeter increase in hind foot length. There is a moderate, positive, significant correlation between juvenile snowshoe hare hind foot length and weight (Pearson’s r = 0.55, p= 8.458368^{-21}). Undisplayed diagnostic plots indicate that the distribution of the residual is hetroscedastic and non-normal, which indicates that although there is a significant linear relationship, the linear model may not be the best fit or important varaibles are missing from the current model.

4. Summary

Key insights from our exploratory data analysis include:

  • On average, juvenile hares observed at the Bonanza Black Spruce site were larger than the other two observation sites.

  • Male juvenile hares are significantly larger in size (specifically weight) in comparison to female juvenile hares.

  • A significant linear relationship between juvenile hare weight and hind foot length can be found. This model follows the equation: weight = 9.52 g mm -1 * (hind foot length) + -279.34g (p=0.02, R2 = 0.299) which indicates an expected increase of 9.52 g for each 1mm increase in hind foot length.

Future analysis surrounding this topic should include:

  • Constructing a multivariate linear regression model to account for potentially signifcant varaibles that were not included in the curent simple linear regression model.
